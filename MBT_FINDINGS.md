# Mini-Brain Toolkit: Implementation Findings

> V6, 2026-07-13.

Findings made while building the toolkit that are not evident from reading the files themselves. Each entry names the options considered and the reason the chosen approach won. Assume the current files are available as ground truth — this document does not restate what they already show.

---

**The namespace token choice carries a generalized lesson.** For a brain whose whole job is to sit beside other brains, the namespace token should be chosen for maximal distinctiveness under co-loading, not just for readability. The specific decision (`MBT` over `BRAIN` or `TOOLKIT`) and its rationale are in `MBT_APPROACH.md` §2.1.

**The two procedure docs are named `MBT_CREATE_BRAIN` and `MBT_CHECK_BRAIN`, not `MBT_ESTABLISH` / `MBT_ASSESS`.** The names moved twice toward obviousness: from precise verbs (`ESTABLISH`/`ASSESS`) to plain verbs (`CREATE`/`CHECK`) to verb-plus-object (`CREATE_BRAIN`/`CHECK_BRAIN`). The driver each time: the audience is an agent scanning a read index for the obvious action, and naming both the action and its object ("create a brain", "check a brain") is what a user actually types — matching the index entry to that intent lowers the chance of the wrong doc being loaded. Precision lost to obviousness on purpose; the fuller framing survives in each doc's body.

**The toolkit must stand on its own as a canonical source: cite external prior art, strip internal lineage.** References to outside research — the agent-memory landscape, ADRs, related systems — are signal and belong in `MBT_RESEARCH.md`. References to *which mini-brain a convention came from*, or to how this toolkit applies the pattern to itself, are redundant and uninteresting to a reader learning the pattern — and they are a live bleed vector: an early `MBT_RESEARCH.md` carried domain-specific examples (a batch of security-vendor acquisitions) verbatim from a sibling brain used as its seed template, content with no bearing on the mini-brain pattern. The rule that follows: distill conventions from exemplar brains, then cut the provenance — the canonical reference and procedure docs state the pattern on its own terms, and any build-time lineage worth keeping lives here in FINDINGS, which exists for exactly that.

**The work-closeout edit classification was preserved when generalizing from its exemplar, not stripped.** `templates/WORK_CLOSEOUT.md` §2 classifies each merge edit four ways (additive / reconcile-supersede / snapshot-replace / append-log), the load-bearing distinction being reconcile-supersede vs. append-log. The exemplar carried this with platform-specific examples that invited deletion during generalization; it was kept because the value lives in that distinction, not the examples. The transferable caution: when distilling a template from one brain, judge a convention by its mechanics, not by whether its illustrations are project-specific.

**"Work item" replaced "feature" as the unit of tracked work.** The setup/closeout bookends and the per-item working set were originally named for *features*, but the machinery (the target walk, the reconcile/append-log classification, the branch→PR→merge→fold lifecycle) is agnostic to whether the work is a feature, a bug fix, or a hardening effort. Naming it "feature" undersold that reach and invited a reader to skip the ritual for non-feature work. Renaming to "work item" (files `WORK_SETUP`/`WORK_CLOSEOUT`, token `<WORK>`, dir `templates/work/`) widened the framing without changing the mechanics. Paired with the rename: setup now defaults to the **full scaffold** ("assume a branch and a PR until proven otherwise" — an oversized scaffold is cheap, under-scaffolding deep work is not), and closeout triggers on the work **concluding** (usually a merge, occasionally a no-code resolution) rather than strictly on merge.
